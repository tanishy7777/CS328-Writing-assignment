# Economy, Energy Mix, and Responsibility in Global CO2 Emissions

This report uses the Our World in Data CO2 dataset to ask why countries differ so much in their emissions. The dataset includes annual CO2 emissions, population, GDP, fuel-specific emissions, energy use, consumption-based emissions, and trade-adjusted emissions. Because this is observational data, the analysis does not prove that one policy directly caused a national emissions path. Instead, it tests three linked claims: richer countries usually emit more per person, energy mix explains why some countries are more carbon-intensive, and production-based accounting can misrepresent who is ultimately responsible for emissions.

The first theme is the economy. In 2022, among countries with population above one million and available GDP data, GDP per capita and CO2 per capita have a strong positive Spearman correlation of about 0.891. This supports the idea that economic development is associated with higher individual carbon footprints. Richer countries tend to have more electricity use, transport, heating and cooling, industrial output, and consumption. However, GDP alone does not explain whether an economy is carbon-efficient. The relationship between GDP per capita and CO2 per GDP is weak, with Spearman correlation about 0.083. This means that rich countries are not automatically dirtier per dollar of output. Some high-income countries have cleaner electricity or more service-based economies, while some fossil-fuel-dependent or industrial economies emit much more CO2 for every unit of GDP. So per-capita emissions and emissions intensity answer different questions: one measures average individual footprint, while the other measures how carbon-heavy production is.

The second theme is energy mix. A country can be wealthy or energy-intensive, but the carbon impact depends heavily on what fuels supply that energy. Using 2024 fuel data, countries where coal makes up at least half of coal, oil, and gas emissions average about 0.274 kg CO2 per kWh of primary energy. Countries where coal contributes less than 20% average about 0.168 kg CO2 per kWh. This gap supports the hypothesis that coal-heavy countries have higher emissions intensity. South Africa, China, India, Vietnam, Kazakhstan, Indonesia, and the Philippines are clear examples of countries where coal dependence pushes energy carbon intensity upward. Energy use per person also strongly predicts CO2 per person: in 2022, energy per capita and CO2 per capita have Spearman correlation about 0.970. But the exceptions are important. Countries with cleaner electricity systems, such as those using more nuclear, hydro, or renewables, can use large amounts of energy without emitting as much CO2 as coal-heavy economies.

The coal result is also supported statistically. A permutation p-test shuffling high-coal and low-coal labels gives p about 0.0004, so a gap this large is unlikely under the null hypothesis that coal share is unrelated to energy emissions intensity. A bootstrap confidence interval for the mean gap is approximately 0.075 to 0.136 kg CO2 per kWh. Since this interval stays above zero, the direction of the result is robust. A CLT simulation using repeated samples of 30 countries shows why sample means can be informative even when country-level emissions are skewed. Finally, k-means clustering on GDP per capita, energy per capita, CO2 per capita, and CO2 per GDP separates countries into four broad profiles: low-income low-energy, middle-income carbon-intensive, high-energy transition mix, and high-income high-footprint. This supports the broader argument that countries are not simply "rich" or "poor"; they differ structurally in energy demand and carbon intensity.

The third theme is policy, trade, and structural change. Production-based emissions count CO2 where it is released, while consumption-based emissions assign emissions to the country whose residents consume the final goods. This changes the responsibility story. In 2023, Switzerland, Singapore, Hong Kong, and Belgium had consumption emissions far above their territorial emissions. Switzerland's trade-adjusted share was about +270%, Singapore's about +266%, and Belgium's about +132%. These countries look cleaner if only territorial emissions are counted, but their consumption depends partly on emissions produced elsewhere. The opposite pattern appears for countries such as Bahrain, Qatar, Vietnam, Kazakhstan, South Africa, Russia, and India, where production emissions exceed consumption emissions. This does not make one accounting method correct and the other wrong. Production accounting tells us where emissions physically occur; consumption accounting tells us who benefits from the goods and services behind those emissions.

Time trends add one more layer. Indexing emissions to 1990 shows that some countries have reduced annual CO2 while others have grown rapidly. By 2024, the United Kingdom and Germany were around 52% and 54% of their 1990 emissions, respectively. The United States was slightly below its 1990 level. China and India, meanwhile, were roughly five times their 1990 emissions. These trends are consistent with differences in policy, technology, industrial structure, energy transition, and development stage, but the OWID CO2 dataset alone cannot isolate the exact cause. The safe conclusion is that structural change matters, and countries follow very different emissions trajectories after industrialization.

Overall, national CO2 emissions are not explained by wealth alone. Economic development is associated with higher per-person emissions, but the final outcome depends on energy demand, fuel mix, trade accounting, and whether a country's emissions path is rising or falling. A country can look high-emitting because it is populous, rich, energy-intensive, coal-heavy, export-oriented, import-dependent, or still industrializing. For climate responsibility, the fairest interpretation combines all three lenses: economic capacity, the carbon intensity of energy systems, and the difference between producing emissions and consuming the goods behind them.
