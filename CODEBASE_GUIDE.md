# Beginner Guide to This Codebase

This project is a data-science writing assignment about global CO2 emissions. It uses the Our World in Data CO2 dataset to answer one big question:

> Why do countries differ so much in CO2 emissions?

The short answer the codebase builds toward is:

> Wealth matters, but emissions are also shaped by energy use, coal dependence, trade, and whether a country's emissions are rising or falling over time.

This is not a machine-learning product or a big software app. It is mostly an analysis notebook, generated figures, written report drafts, and a small static dashboard.

## The Big Story

The project tests three linked ideas:

1. Economy: richer countries usually emit more CO2 per person.
2. Energy mix: coal-heavy countries emit more CO2 for each unit of energy.
3. Responsibility: emissions look different depending on whether you count where CO2 is produced or where the final goods are consumed.

The report is careful about causality. It does not say, "this policy caused this emissions drop." The dataset is observational, meaning it shows real-world patterns but does not run a controlled experiment. So the right language is "associated with," "consistent with," and "suggests," not "proves."

## Data Science Translation

Each row in the main dataset is roughly:

> one country or region in one year.

For example, India in 2022 is one row, India in 2023 is another row, Germany in 2022 is another row, and so on.

Important columns include:

- `country`: country name.
- `iso_code`: country code. The project keeps normal 3-letter country codes and avoids aggregate rows like World.
- `year`: year of the observation.
- `population`: population.
- `gdp`: economic output.
- `co2`: annual territorial CO2 emissions.
- `co2_per_capita`: CO2 per person.
- `co2_per_gdp`: CO2 per unit of economic output.
- `energy_per_capita`: energy use per person.
- `co2_per_unit_energy`: how carbon-heavy the energy system is.
- `coal_co2`, `oil_co2`, `gas_co2`: emissions by fossil fuel type.
- `consumption_co2`: emissions assigned to the people who consume final goods.
- `trade_co2_share`: how much consumption accounting changes a country's emissions.

## How the Notebook Works

The main file is `notebooks/co2_assignment_starter.ipynb`.

It does this sequence:

1. Loads Python libraries:
   - `pandas` for tables.
   - `numpy` for numerical work.
   - `matplotlib` for charts.

2. Reads the dataset:
   - Loads `data/raw/owid-co2-data.csv`.
   - Filters to rows with 3-letter ISO country codes.
   - Creates the `figures/` folder if needed.

3. Checks data coverage:
   - Some columns are available through 2024.
   - Some columns, like GDP or consumption-based emissions, have less complete recent data.
   - So different sections use different latest useful years:
     - 2022 for GDP and energy per person.
     - 2023 for trade and consumption emissions.
     - 2024 for fuel mix.

4. Defines helper statistics:
   - Spearman correlation.
   - Permutation tests.
   - Mean-difference permutation tests.

5. Runs method checks:
   - Permutation test for the coal gap.
   - Bootstrap interval for the coal gap.
   - Central Limit Theorem simulation.
   - K-means clustering.

6. Tests the three hypotheses:
   - H1 economy.
   - H2 energy mix.
   - H3 trade and responsibility.

7. Saves charts into `figures/`.

## Hypothesis 1: Economy

Question:

> Do richer countries emit more?

The notebook calculates:

```text
GDP per capita = GDP / population
```

That means "economic output per person."

Then it compares GDP per capita with two emissions measures:

- `co2_per_capita`: emissions per person.
- `co2_per_gdp`: emissions per unit of GDP.

Main result:

- GDP per person and CO2 per person are strongly related.
- GDP per person and CO2 per GDP are barely related.

Plain-English meaning:

Rich countries usually have higher individual carbon footprints because people consume more energy, transportation, goods, heating, cooling, and services. But rich countries are not always dirtier per dollar of economic output. Some rich countries have cleaner electricity or service-heavy economies.

Important distinction:

```text
CO2 per capita = average individual footprint
CO2 per GDP = how carbon-heavy the economy is
```

Those are different questions.

## Hypothesis 2: Energy Mix

Question:

> Does coal make a country's energy system more carbon-intensive?

The notebook creates:

```text
known_fossil_co2 = coal_co2 + oil_co2 + gas_co2
coal_share = coal_co2 / known_fossil_co2
```

Then it compares:

- high-coal countries: coal share at least 50%.
- low-coal countries: coal share below 20%.

Main result:

High-coal countries have higher `co2_per_unit_energy`.

Plain-English meaning:

Two countries can use similar amounts of energy, but if one gets much more of that energy from coal, it usually emits more CO2 per unit of energy.

The notebook also compares:

```text
energy_per_capita vs co2_per_capita
```

That relationship is very strong. In simple terms:

> The more energy people use, the more CO2 they usually emit.

But the exceptions matter. A country using nuclear, hydro, renewables, or lower-carbon fuels can use lots of energy without emitting as much as a coal-heavy country.

## Hypothesis 3: Trade and Responsibility

Question:

> Who is responsible for emissions: the country that produces goods, or the country that consumes them?

The project compares two accounting methods:

- Territorial or production-based CO2: emissions counted where CO2 is physically released.
- Consumption-based CO2: emissions counted where the final goods are consumed.

Example:

If a factory in Country A makes products for shoppers in Country B:

- production accounting charges Country A.
- consumption accounting charges Country B.

Main result:

Some rich importing economies look cleaner under territorial accounting than under consumption accounting. Some exporter/manufacturing countries look dirtier under territorial accounting than under consumption accounting.

Plain-English meaning:

Counting method changes the responsibility story. Neither method is automatically "the correct one." They answer different questions.

```text
Production accounting = where emissions happen
Consumption accounting = who benefits from the goods
```

The notebook also makes a time-trend graph indexed to 1990:

```text
1990 emissions = 100
later value = 100 * current emissions / 1990 emissions
```

This makes countries comparable even if one country is much larger than another. It asks:

> Has this country gone up or down compared with its own 1990 baseline?

## The Stats, Without the Pain

### Spearman Correlation

Spearman correlation checks whether two variables move in the same rank order.

It asks:

> When countries rank higher on X, do they also tend to rank higher on Y?

Why use it here?

Country emissions data has huge outliers. Qatar, the United States, China, India, and small fossil-fuel economies can distort normal linear correlation. Spearman is more robust because it focuses on ranking.

How to read it:

```text
+1   strong upward relationship
 0   no clear rank relationship
-1   strong downward relationship
```

Examples from the project:

- GDP per capita vs CO2 per capita: about `0.891`, very strong.
- Energy per capita vs CO2 per capita: about `0.970`, extremely strong.
- GDP per capita vs CO2 per GDP: about `0.083`, weak.

### Permutation Test

A permutation test asks:

> If the labels were random, how often would we see a result this extreme?

For the coal section, the real labels are "high coal" and "low coal." The test shuffles those labels thousands of times.

If shuffled data almost never creates a gap as large as the real gap, then the real gap is probably not just random noise.

The output is a p-value.

Plain-English p-value:

> How surprising is this result if there is actually no relationship?

Small p-values mean "pretty surprising under randomness."

### Bootstrap Confidence Interval

Bootstrap means:

> Resample the data many times and see how much the result changes.

The notebook repeatedly samples high-coal and low-coal countries with replacement, recalculates the mean gap, and looks at the middle 95% of those simulated gaps.

If the whole interval is above zero, the project treats the coal effect as directionally stable.

### Central Limit Theorem Demo

The Central Limit Theorem says sample averages often behave more smoothly than individual data points.

Country emissions are messy and skewed. But if you repeatedly sample 30 countries and calculate the average, those averages form a smoother distribution.

This does not make outliers disappear. It just explains why averages and confidence intervals can still be useful.

### K-means Clustering

K-means groups countries into clusters based on similar numbers.

This project uses four features:

- log GDP per capita.
- log energy per capita.
- CO2 per capita.
- CO2 per GDP.

The clusters are descriptive, not moral rankings and not proof of causation.

The dashboard labels them as:

- Low income, low energy.
- Middle income, carbon intensive.
- High energy transition mix.
- High income, high footprint.

## The Static Website

The `site/` folder is a standalone dashboard.

It does not run Python. It uses:

- `index.html` for page layout.
- `styles.css` for design.
- `app.js` for interactive charts.
- Chart.js from a CDN for chart rendering.
- `site/data/co2-dashboard.json` as already-prepared data.

When the browser opens the site:

1. `index.html` loads Chart.js and `app.js`.
2. `app.js` fetches `data/co2-dashboard.json`.
3. It fills dropdowns with country names.
4. It draws scatterplots, bar charts, line charts, and method charts.
5. When the user changes a dropdown, the relevant chart is destroyed and redrawn.

The dashboard has panels for:

- H1 Economy.
- H2 Energy Mix.
- H3 Responsibility.
- Methods Appendix.

## What to Say in a Submission

A solid one-paragraph summary would be:

> This project analyzes the Our World in Data CO2 dataset to understand why national emissions differ. The analysis finds that GDP per capita is strongly associated with CO2 per capita, but GDP alone does not explain carbon intensity. Energy use per person is the strongest predictor of per-person emissions, while coal-heavy energy systems produce more CO2 per unit of energy. Finally, production-based and consumption-based accounting change which countries appear most responsible, showing that emissions responsibility depends on both where goods are made and where they are consumed. Because the data is observational, the report describes associations and structural patterns rather than claiming direct policy causation.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- Do not say the dataset proves policy caused emissions to fall.
- Do not confuse total emissions with per-person emissions.
- Do not confuse per-person emissions with CO2 per GDP.
- Do not treat production accounting and consumption accounting as interchangeable.
- Do not say clustering proves country types are objectively real categories.
- Do not use 2024 for every section, because not all variables have good 2024 coverage.

## Mental Model

Think of each country as having four layers:

```text
Economy:       how rich and productive is it?
Energy demand: how much energy do people use?
Fuel mix:      is that energy coal-heavy or cleaner?
Accounting:    are emissions produced locally or imported through goods?
```

The whole codebase is just a structured way of showing that all four layers matter.
