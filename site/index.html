Economy raises individual footprint, but not efficiency by itself

GDP per capita strongly tracks CO2 per capita, while GDP per capita barely tracks CO2 per GDP. The richer-versus-poorer story is real, but incomplete.

Coal-heavy systems emit more CO2 for each unit of energy

Countries with coal above half of fossil CO2 have much higher carbon intensity than countries with low coal dependence. Energy demand matters, but fuel mix decides how dirty it becomes.

Consumption accounting changes who looks responsible

Importer economies can look cleaner under territorial accounting. Exporters and manufacturing-heavy economies often show the opposite pattern.

Emissions paths diverge after 1990

Germany and the United Kingdom fell to nearly half of their 1990 emissions by 2024, while China and India grew roughly fivefold. This is consistent with structural change, not proof of one cause.

Statistical checks make the story defensible

The dashboard uses non-parametric tests and resampling because country emissions are skewed and outlier-heavy. K-means gives an exploratory grouping of country profiles, not a causal model.

Final argument

National CO2 emissions are not explained by wealth alone. Economic development is associated with higher per-person emissions, but the final outcome depends on energy demand, coal dependence, trade accounting, and whether a country's emissions path is rising or falling.
