CS 328 Data Story

Economy, Energy Mix, and Responsibility in Global CO2 Emissions

An analysis of how wealth, energy systems, and trade accounting affect national CO2 emissions.

OWID dataset

Central hypothesis

National CO2 emissions are shaped by wealth, but the clearest differences come from energy demand, fuel mix, trade accounting, and structural change.

The data can show strong patterns, but it cannot prove that one policy caused a specific country's emissions path. We use three linked hypotheses to keep the comparison focused.

H1
Economy

Higher GDP per capita is associated with higher CO2 per capita, but GDP alone does not explain CO2 per GDP.

H2
Energy mix

Coal-heavy countries have higher CO2 intensity, while energy use per capita strongly predicts CO2 per capita.

H3
Responsibility

Production and consumption accounting change who looks responsible, and country emissions paths diverge after 1990.

Methods

We used rank correlation, permutation testing, bootstrap uncertainty, and exploratory clustering to check the main patterns.

  • Spearman rhandles skewed country rankings
  • Permutationtests whether the coal gap is surprising
  • Bootstrapestimates uncertainty without normality
  • K-meanssummarizes country profiles, not causality

Hypothesis 1

Economy raises individual footprint, but not carbon efficiency by itself.

GDP per capita strongly tracks CO2 per capita, while GDP per capita barely tracks CO2 per GDP. The richer-versus-poorer story is real, but incomplete.

Test

Compare GDP per capita with CO2 per capita and CO2 per GDP for countries above one million people.

Evidence

Spearman r is 0.891 for GDP versus CO2 per person, but only 0.083 for GDP versus CO2 per GDP.

Interpretation

Per-capita emissions measure individual footprint; CO2 per GDP measures production efficiency.

Hypothesis 2

Energy demand predicts emissions, and coal decides how carbon-heavy that demand becomes.

Countries with coal above half of fossil CO2 have much higher energy carbon intensity than low-coal countries. Cleaner energy mixes create important exceptions.

Test

Compare coal share against CO2 per unit energy, then compare energy use per capita against CO2 per capita.

Evidence

High-coal countries average 0.106 kg CO2/kWh more than low-coal countries, with a bootstrap interval above zero.

Interpretation

Demand explains how much energy people use; fuel mix explains why similar demand can produce different emissions.

Hypothesis 3

Responsibility changes with accounting method, and national trajectories split after 1990.

Production-based accounting shows where CO2 enters the atmosphere. Consumption-based accounting shows who benefits from the goods behind those emissions. Time trends show structural divergence, not a single universal path.

Test

Compare trade-adjusted emissions in 2023 and index selected countries to their 1990 emissions baseline.

Evidence

Switzerland, Singapore, and Belgium rise under consumption accounting, while exporters often move the other way.

Interpretation

The dataset supports careful structural claims; it does not prove that one policy caused one country's path.

Final argument

A fair emissions interpretation needs all three lenses.

Economic capacity helps explain who can consume more energy, fuel mix explains how carbon-intensive that energy is, and trade accounting shows who benefits from emissions that may occur somewhere else.
