Economy, Energy Mix, and Responsibility in Global CO2 Emissions

A guided dashboard for explaining why national CO2 responsibility cannot be reduced to one number.

OWID dataset

Main argument

Wealth matters, but the emissions story needs three lenses.

Richer countries usually emit more per person. But carbon intensity depends on the energy system, and responsibility changes when emissions are counted by consumption instead of only by production.

Economy raises individual footprint, but not efficiency by itself

GDP per capita strongly tracks CO2 per capita, while GDP per capita barely tracks CO2 per GDP. The richer-versus-poorer story is real, but incomplete.

Hypothesis

Higher GDP per person should be associated with higher emissions per person.

How to read this

Move left to right for richer countries. The vertical position shows either individual footprint or emissions per dollar of GDP.

Takeaway

The first view slopes upward strongly. The second view is much flatter, showing that wealth alone does not decide carbon efficiency.

Coal-heavy systems emit more CO2 for each unit of energy

Countries with coal above half of fossil CO2 have much higher carbon intensity than countries with low coal dependence. Energy demand matters, but fuel mix decides how dirty it becomes.

Hypothesis

Coal-heavy countries should have higher CO2 intensity because coal releases more CO2 per unit of useful energy.

How to read this

The coal view compares fuel structure with energy intensity. The energy view compares total energy use with personal emissions.

Takeaway

Demand explains how much energy people use; fuel mix explains why similar demand can produce different emissions.

Consumption accounting changes who looks responsible

Importer economies can look cleaner under territorial accounting. Exporters and manufacturing-heavy economies often show the opposite pattern.

Hypothesis

Production-based and consumption-based emissions should change which countries look more responsible.

How to read this

Positive bars mean consumption emissions are higher than territorial emissions. Negative bars mean a country produces more CO2 than its residents consume directly.

Takeaway

Territorial accounting tells where CO2 enters the atmosphere; consumption accounting shows who benefits from the goods behind it.

Emissions paths diverge after 1990

Germany and the United Kingdom fell to nearly half of their 1990 emissions by 2024, while China and India grew roughly fivefold. This is consistent with structural change, not proof of one cause.

Question

Do countries follow one common emissions path after industrialization?

How to read this

The index view makes countries comparable by setting each country to 100 in 1990. Values below 100 mean emissions fell.

Takeaway

Some wealthy countries reduced annual CO2, while rapidly industrializing countries increased sharply. The dataset shows divergence, not one simple cause.

Statistical checks make the story defensible

The dashboard uses non-parametric tests and resampling because country emissions are skewed and outlier-heavy. K-means gives an exploratory grouping of country profiles, not a causal model.

Why methods matter

Country emissions are uneven and outlier-heavy, so the analysis should not rely only on chart shapes.

How to read this

Permutation asks whether the observed gap is surprising. Bootstrap estimates uncertainty. CLT shows sampling behavior. K-means summarizes profiles.

Takeaway

The coal-intensity result remains positive under resampling, and country profiles are more complex than a rich-versus-poor split.

Final argument

National CO2 emissions are not explained by wealth alone. Economic development is associated with higher per-person emissions, but the final outcome depends on energy demand, coal dependence, trade accounting, and whether a country's emissions path is rising or falling.
