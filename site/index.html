CS 328 Data Story

Economy, Energy Mix, and Responsibility in Global CO2 Emissions

A blog-style walkthrough of the notebook analysis: why countries emit differently, why accounting choices change responsibility, and why economic growth does not automatically mean emissions fall.

OWID dataset

Research question

What actually explains national CO2 emissions?

The simple answer would be "rich countries emit more." The notebook shows that this is only the starting point. GDP per capita is strongly associated with CO2 per person, but the final emissions story also depends on energy use, coal dependence, imported consumption, land-use change, historical responsibility, and whether economic growth has separated from territorial CO2.

Because this is an observational country-year dataset, the analysis avoids claiming that one policy directly caused one country's path. Instead, it builds a careful descriptive argument: emissions are shaped by economic capacity, but national responsibility changes depending on whether we measure production, consumption, land use, history, or transition progress.

Data and scope

The dataset is broad, but each question needs its own clean sample.

The analysis uses the Our World in Data CO2 dataset, which includes country-year observations for territorial emissions, population, GDP, energy use, fuel-specific emissions, consumption-based emissions, cumulative CO2, and land-use change. The notebook first removes aggregate regions and obvious duplicate rows, then applies hypothesis-specific filters.

This local filtering matters. GDP coverage is strongest around 2022, trade-adjusted consumption data is broadest in 2023, and fuel or land-use variables extend to 2024. A single blanket missing-value rule would throw away useful evidence, so each hypothesis states its year and variables separately.

Exploratory analysis

The baseline is concentration, not equality.

Before testing hypotheses, the notebook checks the shape of the global problem. Total emissions are concentrated among a relatively small number of large national systems, while per-capita emissions tell a different story. Population explains some of the scale difference, but residuals show that size alone cannot explain national emissions.

The source-composition and correlation checks motivate the rest of the report: energy use, fuel mix, GDP, and accounting method are related, but they are not interchangeable. That is why the story moves through economy, energy systems, accounting, land use, historical responsibility, and decoupling.

Part I: Foundation

Economy raises footprint, but energy systems decide how carbon-heavy that footprint becomes.

H1 asks whether wealth explains emissions. In 2022, GDP per capita and CO2 per capita have a strong positive rank relationship. Richer countries usually have higher electricity use, transport demand, industrial output, heating and cooling, and consumption. This supports H1A.

H1B is the important caveat: GDP per capita barely tracks CO2 per GDP. A rich country is not automatically carbon-intensive per unit of output. Some high-income countries have cleaner electricity or more service-heavy economies, while some fossil-fuel-dependent economies emit much more for each unit of GDP.

H2 adds the mechanism. Energy use per person is extremely strongly associated with CO2 per person, but the fuel mix decides whether that energy demand becomes especially carbon-heavy. Countries where coal makes up at least half of fossil CO2 have much higher CO2 per unit energy than low-coal countries. Cleaner electricity systems create exceptions: a country can use substantial energy without having the same emissions intensity as a coal-heavy system.

Statistical checks

The checks support the story, but each one has a specific interpretation.

The notebook does not rely only on charts. It uses rank correlation, a permutation test, bootstrap uncertainty, a CLT simulation, and k-means clustering. These checks are descriptive safeguards: they make the visual patterns more defensible without turning the dataset into a causal experiment.

  • Spearman correlation: GDP and energy variables are skewed, so rank correlation checks whether countries preserve a monotonic ordering rather than assuming a clean linear relationship.
  • Permutation test: The coal gap is unlikely to appear if high-coal and low-coal labels were randomly assigned, so the fuel-mix result is not just a visual coincidence.
  • Bootstrap interval: The high-coal minus low-coal intensity gap stays above zero across resamples, so the direction of the result is stable.
  • CLT simulation: Country-level emissions are skewed, but repeated sample means are smoother. Averages are useful, but outliers still matter.
  • K-means clustering: Countries fall into profiles such as low-income low-energy, middle-income carbon-intensive, high-energy transition mix, and high-income high-footprint. The clusters summarize structure; they do not prove causality.

Part II: Accounting

Responsibility changes when the metric changes.

H3 shifts from emissions production to responsibility accounting. Territorial emissions count CO2 where it enters the atmosphere. Consumption-based emissions assign those emissions to the country whose residents consume the final goods. Switzerland, Singapore, and Belgium look very different under this lens because consumption emissions exceed territorial emissions.

H4 adds land-use change. Fossil-fuel CO2 is not the only emissions pathway. In countries with major forest loss, peatland disturbance, agricultural expansion, or land restoration, CO2 including land-use change can be much higher or lower than fossil-only CO2. This does not replace the energy story; it prevents the report from pretending national emissions are only about industry and electricity.

H5 separates current annual emissions from cumulative historical responsibility. Current emissions identify where reductions matter now. Cumulative emissions ask who contributed most to the stock of atmospheric CO2. These questions overlap, but they are not identical, so a fair interpretation needs both.

Part III: Transition

Decoupling tests whether growth can separate from emissions.

H6 asks the hardest question in the notebook. A country can become richer while reducing CO2 per unit GDP, but this is only relative decoupling if total emissions still rise. Absolute decoupling is stricter: GDP grows while territorial CO2 falls.

From 1990 to 2022, many countries reduced carbon intensity, and many achieved relative decoupling. Absolute decoupling is less common. That distinction matters because efficiency gains help, but climate progress ultimately requires the absolute emissions curve to fall.

Conclusion

A fair emissions interpretation needs all six lenses.

National CO2 emissions are not explained by wealth alone. Economic development is associated with higher per-person emissions, but final responsibility depends on energy demand, fuel mix, trade accounting, land-use change, historical responsibility, and whether growth has actually separated from emissions.

A country can look high-emitting because it is populous, rich, energy-intensive, coal-heavy, export-oriented, import-dependent, land-use intensive, historically industrialized, or still developing. The strongest conclusion is therefore not a single-cause explanation. It is a layered interpretation: climate responsibility changes depending on which emissions question we ask.
